Recovery score (0–100%)
WHOOP's core daily metric — a composite of HRV, resting heart rate, sleep performance, and respiratory rate. It tells you how ready your body is to handle physiological stress today.

Green (67–100%): Body is primed. Push hard if your training calls for it.
Yellow (34–66%): Moderate readiness. Stick to maintenance intensity.
Red (0–33%): Physiological signal to rest. Don't override it with willpower.
Trend matters more than a single day. Watch your 7-day rolling average to spot patterns of under-recovery.
Heart rate variability (HRV)
HRV measures the variation in time between your heartbeats (milliseconds). A higher HRV signals a more adaptable autonomic nervous system — meaning your body has recovered well and is ready for stress.

WHOOP calculates HRV during your deepest sleep using the rMSSD method. Your personal baseline matters far more than absolute numbers — WHOOP compares you to your own 30-day rolling average, not population norms.
HRV drops sharply after alcohol, illness, poor sleep, and travel. It's your body's earliest stress signal — often showing up 24–48 hours before you feel it.
Resting heart rate (RHR)
Your RHR is measured during your deepest sleep phase — the most accurate window. Lower RHR generally indicates better cardiovascular fitness and recovery.

WHOOP flags deviations from your personal baseline. An elevated RHR of 5+ bpm often precedes illness or overtraining by 1–2 days — catching it before you feel the symptoms.

Trained athletes: 40–60 bpm  |  General population: 60–100 bpm
RHR elevated by 5+ bpm above baseline? Scale back training intensity that day — your body is already under stress.
Blood oxygen saturation (SpO₂)
SpO₂ measures what percentage of your hemoglobin is carrying oxygen. Normal healthy range is 95–100%. WHOOP measures this during sleep, when breathing disruptions are most detectable.

Consistent drops below 94% may indicate sleep apnea, altitude effects, or respiratory issues.
Consistently below 94%? That warrants a conversation with your doctor — could be undetected sleep apnea.

Sleep performance
The percentage of your personalized sleep need that you actually fulfilled. WHOOP calculates your individual sleep need daily based on your body, activity level, and accumulated debt — it's not a fixed 8 hours.

85%+: Peaked — body got what it needed.
70–85%: Green — adequate recovery.
Below 70%: Sleep debt building. Expect lower recovery score tomorrow.
Even one night under 70% performance measurably lowers HRV the following morning.
Time asleep vs sleep need
WHOOP personalizes your sleep need daily — it increases after hard training days and decreases after rest days. It accounts for accumulated debt, recent sleep quality, and physiological strain.

This is why two people can both sleep 7 hours and have very different recovery scores — their needs are different.
If your sleep need is consistently rising week over week, you're accumulating a debt that's quietly degrading your performance.
Sleep efficiency
The ratio of actual sleep time to total time in bed. Ideal range: 85–100%. Below 85% means fragmented sleep — you're in bed but not fully recovering. Common causes: awakenings, restlessness, long sleep onset, or alcohol.

Sleep efficiency is often a more actionable metric than duration — you can't always sleep longer, but you can sleep better.
Avoid screens, alcohol, and heavy meals within 2 hours of bed to meaningfully improve efficiency.
Sleep latency
How long it took you to fall asleep after getting into bed. Ideal: 10–20 minutes.

Under 5 min: You're overtired — your body crashed from exhaustion.
10–20 min: Ideal. Relaxed and naturally ready to sleep.
Over 30 min: Anxiety, caffeine sensitivity, or poor sleep hygiene.

WHOOP detects sleep onset via heart rate drop — no button to press.
Consistent latency over 20 min? Try moving bedtime 30 min earlier and cutting caffeine after 2pm.

Day strain (0–21 scale)
Cardiovascular load — how hard your heart worked throughout the full day. Calculated from time spent in each heart rate zone, not just workouts.

0–9: Light / recovery activity
10–13: Moderate — maintenance level
14–17: High — pushing limits
18–21: All out — maximum effort

The key rule: match strain to your recovery score. High recovery + high strain = productive adaptation. Low recovery + high strain = overtraining and breakdown.
Strain slightly above your recovery-recommended level is the sweet spot for adaptation without burnout.
Total calorie burn
Your total daily energy expenditure (TDEE) — basal metabolic rate plus all activity calories. Calculated using continuous HR data and your body measurements (weight, height, age) for higher accuracy than step-based trackers.
Underfueling after high-strain days is one of the top recovery mistakes athletes make. Eat to match your output.
Max heart rate
The highest HR recorded in the last 24 hours. Time spent near your personal max HR is what drives strain most. WHOOP estimates your true max from measured data over time — more accurate than the 220-minus-age formula.
Average heart rate
Mean HR across the full 24-hour day. An average higher than your normal — even without deliberate exercise — is a strong signal of physiological stress from illness, travel, anxiety, or overtraining. It's one of the best passive stress indicators WHOOP tracks.

What each sleep stage does for your body
Deep sleep (SWS) — the most important for athletes: Physical repair happens here. Human growth hormone (HGH) is released, muscles rebuild, immune function strengthens. Target: 15–20% of total sleep.

REM — the cognitive stage: Memory consolidation, emotional regulation, creativity. Where learning gets locked in. Target: 20–25% of total sleep.

Light sleep — transition and organization: Your brain organizes the day's information. Necessary but less impactful than SWS or REM.

Awake — brief is normal: Short awakenings throughout the night are physiologically normal. Under 10% of sleep time in "awake" is healthy.
Alcohol is the biggest destroyer of deep sleep — even 1–2 drinks can reduce SWS by 20–30%. The effect shows clearly in your WHOOP scores the next morning.

Respiratory rate
Breaths per minute during sleep. Normal range: 12–20 rpm. A rise of 1–2 rpm above your personal baseline is WHOOP's earliest illness detection signal — it often appears 12–24 hours before you feel unwell.

WHOOP detects this through subtle patterns in continuous heart rate monitoring during sleep.
Respiratory rate spike + low HRV together = your body is likely fighting something. Consider skipping hard training that day.
Sleep consistency
How consistent your sleep and wake times are across the past week. Your circadian rhythm is regulated by consistent timing — irregular schedules reduce sleep quality even when total hours look adequate.

A high consistency score (80%+) means your body knows when to expect sleep and prepares accordingly — leading to faster sleep onset and more restorative cycles.
Even on weekends, staying within 30 minutes of your usual sleep/wake time dramatically improves recovery quality.

Heart rate zones — what they mean for your training
Zone 1 — Light50–60% max HR. Warm-up, cool-down, active recovery. Minimal cardiovascular strain. Good for recovery days.
Zone 2 — Moderate60–70% max HR. Aerobic base building, fat oxidation, long-term endurance foundation. The most underrated training zone.
Zone 3 — Hard70–80% max HR. Lactate threshold training. Pushes your aerobic ceiling. Heavy contribution to strain score.
Zone 4 — Very hard80–90% max HR. High intensity intervals. Very high strain contribution. Requires adequate recovery between sessions.
Zone 5 — Max90%+ max HR. Sprint or all-out effort. Drives highest strain. Full recovery needed — don't visit this zone on yellow or red recovery days.
Strain vs recovery — the fundamental rule
How to match your training to your body's signals
Green recovery (67–100%) + high strain (14–21): Productive. Your body is ready to adapt to the stress you're applying. This is where gains happen.

Yellow recovery (34–66%) + moderate strain (10–13): Sustainable. Maintain fitness without pushing into overtraining territory.

Red recovery (0–33%) + high strain: Overtraining. You're applying stress to a body that hasn't recovered — this leads to accumulated fatigue, injury risk, and performance decline.

Red recovery + low strain: Correct response. Use this as an active recovery day — walk, stretch, breathe. Let your body rebuild.
The athletes who improve fastest aren't the ones who train hardest every day — they're the ones who time their hardest days to their highest recovery.
